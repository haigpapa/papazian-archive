## Two Modes, One Investigation

**The Autopsy** and **Beirut Phantom** are companion projects sharing a methodology: both use photogrammetry — the construction of 3D models from photographs — to rebuild spaces from documentary evidence, and both treat the incompleteness of that reconstruction as content rather than limitation.

---

## The Autopsy (تشريح)

An autopsy is performed on a body that is no longer living, to determine what happened to it and why. The Autopsy applies this methodology to a city — specifically Beirut, specifically the versions of it that existed before the 2020 port explosion, before the civil war, and in the memories of the diaspora who cannot return to verify their recollections against reality.

The project uses photogrammetry to rebuild spaces from documentary evidence: personal photographs, satellite imagery, archival footage, eyewitness description. The resulting environments are not accurate reconstructions; they are evidence-based conjectures, assembled from partial data, visibly incomplete, refusing the false certainty of photorealistic rendering.

**The gaps in the model are as significant as the data that fills them.**

Structured as AI-augmented interactive fiction in the tradition of digital archaeology games (Her Story, Disco Elysium). The user navigates a reconstructed space and assembles evidence about what happened to it. The investigation is never resolved — the autopsy reveals cause of death but not meaning.

> *The building still stands. It is not the same building. The question the project asks is: what does it mean to return to a place that has been rebuilt on top of itself? Who owns the version that existed before?*

---

## Beirut Phantom

A VR/browser installation using photogrammetry scans of specific Beirut locations — pre-explosion, post-explosion, current — layered as temporal strata. The viewer navigates between historical states of the same physical space.

The layering is visible: the ghosts of previous versions are present in the current one. The 1975 version of a building exists as a transparent overlay on the 2019 version, which exists as a layer beneath the 2021 reconstruction. History is not linear; it is spatial. The city is a palimpsest; the installation renders it as one.

---

## Why Incomplete Reconstruction Is the Point

The temptation with photogrammetric reconstruction is photorealism — to produce a model indistinguishable from a photograph of the original space. This is technically impressive and conceptually wrong for this project.

A photorealistic reconstruction of a destroyed building implies that the destruction can be undone — that reconstruction is a sufficient response to loss. It is not.

The Autopsy / Beirut Phantom embraces the gaps in the reconstruction as content:
- Where data is sparse, the model is wireframe
- Where memory is uncertain, surfaces are translucent
- Where the record has been deliberately destroyed, the space is void

The incompleteness of the model mirrors the incompleteness of what is available to know — and refuses to supplement what is known with what the model would prefer to render.

## The AI Layer

The most compelling digital archaeology experiences — Her Story, Device 6, NieR: Automata — are compelling because they put the player in the position of assembling meaning from fragments, with no guarantee that the assembled meaning is correct.

This is structurally identical to the experience of diasporic memory: assembling a coherent account of a place and a past from fragments that are partial, conflicting, and mediated through layers of transmission.

The Autopsy's AI layer is designed to inhabit this uncertainty — producing responses that are plausible but not authoritative, that extend the investigation without resolving it.

---

## Bartlett Thesis Lineage

The intellectual lineage is direct. The Bartlett thesis "Architecture in Low Res" examined how digital tools like Google Earth mediate our understanding of built space — how the "low resolution" of a digital proxy alters perception of the physical reality it represents.

Beirut Phantom is the thesis made operational: a designed environment that is deliberately low-resolution, that treats the gap between digital representation and physical reality as its primary subject, and that asks what it means for a diaspora to navigate a city they know only through digital proxies.

The 15-year thread: Bartlett thesis (2013–2015) → Beirut Phantom (2025+).
