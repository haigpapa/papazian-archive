## The Argument

Before drone photography normalized the aerial view, before Street View made every street a navigable image, before satellite imagery gave anyone with a browser the sensation of seeing from above — architects drew buildings. They drew plans, sections, elevations. These drawings were not representations of buildings; they were arguments about buildings, made in the language of geometry.

*Architecture in Low Res* examines what happens when those arguments are replaced by photographs, satellite imagery, and digital proxies — when the abstraction that architectural drawing required is replaced by the apparent directness of the photographic image.

The thesis argues that this replacement is not neutral. Digital tools like Google Earth and Street View do not give us unmediated access to buildings; they give us a specific kind of mediated access, shaped by resolution constraints, compression artifacts, viewpoint limitations, and the particular aesthetic of satellite and street-level photography. The "low resolution" of these digital proxies is not a technical limitation to be overcome — it is a new epistemological condition that changes how we understand what buildings are and what they do.

**The "glitch" — the artifact, the pixelation, the gap between digital representation and physical reality — is not error. It is information about the nature of the mediation.**

## Why This Matters Now

The thesis was written in 2013–2015, before "digital twin," "computational design," and "photogrammetric reconstruction" entered common architectural vocabulary. The questions it asked have since become urgent across multiple fields:

- How does a digital representation of a building relate to the building itself?
- What is lost in the translation from physical to digital?
- What new knowledge becomes possible when that translation is embraced rather than minimized?
- When the resolution of our digital proxy is inadequate to the complexity of the physical reality, what do we do with the gap?

These are not only architectural questions. They are the questions that DERIVE asks about memory. They are the questions that Beirut Phantom asks about the destroyed city. They are the questions that the Localization Gap research asks about AI-generated culture.

## The Lineage

The intellectual thread from "Architecture in Low Res" runs directly through the current practice:

| Project | Connection |
|---------|-----------|
| **Beirut Phantom** | Direct operationalization — a designed environment that is deliberately low-resolution, treating the gap between digital representation and physical reality as its primary subject |

| **HAH-WAS** | The Localization Gap as a "low resolution" problem — AI models lack the resolution to capture the microtonal nuances of non-Western music |

The thesis is not a historical artifact. It is the theoretical seed of everything that came after.

## Bartlett Context

The Bartlett School of Architecture at UCL is one of the most intellectually rigorous architectural schools in the world. The program that Haig attended — MA Architectural History & Criticism — positioned architectural theory in relation to philosophy, cultural studies, and media theory, rather than as a subset of design practice. The thesis emerged from sustained engagement with theorists including:

- Bernard Cache (objectile, pre-individual)
- Rem Koolhaas (Generic City, junkspace)
- Mark Fisher (hauntology, capitalist realism)
- The psychogeographic tradition (Debord, Situationism)

The psychogeographic thread — particularly the concept of the *dérive* as a methodology for navigating urban space guided by affective rather than functional logic — became the direct precursor to DERIVE's drift algorithm.

## Bourj Hammoud Undergraduate Thesis (AUB, 2004–2009)

*Note: This document also serves as an appropriate location to note the undergraduate thesis at the American University of Beirut, which predates the Bartlett work.*

The AUB thesis examined the Armenian neighborhood of Bourj Hammoud in Beirut — mapping its landmarks, documenting its displacement, and analyzing its urban fabric as a constructed cultural memory. A neighborhood built by genocide survivors to approximate the places they had lost, populated with street names referencing cities in Turkey that no longer had Armenian populations.

This early work established the core methodological concern that persists across the entire practice: **how do communities encode cultural memory in built space, and what happens to that encoding when the space is destroyed or transformed?**

The Bourj Hammoud thesis is the origin point. Beirut Phantom is the current iteration. DERIVE is the computational generalization.
