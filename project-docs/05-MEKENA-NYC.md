# MEKENA NYC
## Adaptive Reuse as Cultural Infrastructure

**Tier:** Lead Project  
**Type:** Architectural Social System / Physical Operating System  
**Location:** Dutch Kills, Queens, New York City  
**Status:** Active Development  
**Funding:** $50,000 initial capital secured  
**Energy Performance:** 30% reduction achieved  

---

## The Name

*Mekena* — from Arabic: مكان (makan) — place, location, the specific here that grounds you.

The word carries its own argument: that even the most displaced person is always somewhere. The question is whether that somewhere is livable.

---

## The Building

Architecture makes claims about who belongs in a space. The standard categories — residential, commercial, institutional — assume a stable subject: someone who lives somewhere, works somewhere, belongs to an institution. MEKENA is built for people who don't fit those categories.

MEKENA NYC is an adaptive reuse project converting a 1920s brick residential building in the Dutch Kills neighborhood of Queens into a live/work residency for queer, immigrant, and exiled artists. It is simultaneously a physical renovation, a programming institution, and a proof-of-concept for a specific model of cultural infrastructure — one that treats affordability, sustainability, and cultural specificity as non-negotiable design constraints rather than aspirational add-ons.

---

## Why Queens, Why Dutch Kills

Queens is one of the most linguistically diverse urban areas on earth. Dutch Kills sits at the junction of several immigrant communities whose artistic practices are underrepresented in the institutional geography of New York. MEKENA is designed to be in that neighborhood specifically, not in spite of its distance from Manhattan's arts infrastructure but because of it.

---

## Design Logic

| Design Layer | Constraint | Implementation |
|-------------|------------|----------------|
| **Structural** | Historic 1920s brick envelope — must be preserved | Interior reconfigured for adaptable studio/residence configurations. The constraint of the existing structure becomes the design logic: new systems run through existing bones. |
| **Sustainability** | 30% energy reduction target (achieved) | Sustainable materials, green technology integration, IoT environmental monitoring. ESG-aligned infrastructure. |
| **Programming** | 2–3 artist residencies annually, public-facing events | Exhibitions, workshops, performances, community meals, oral history projects, urban farming. The building's programming is as much a design decision as its floor plan. |
| **Governance** | Non-extractive model | Artists in residence participate in governance decisions. The space is not a service delivered to artists but a system co-created with them. |

---

## The Architecture Argument

The standard model for supporting displaced artists in New York is the temporary residency: a few months of subsidized space, a cohort of fellows, a final exhibition, dissolution. This model produces individual careers. It does not produce communities.

MEKENA is designed around a different premise: that cultural communities require permanent infrastructure to survive. Not a program that can be defunded but a building that exists. Not a cohort that disbands but a neighborhood that accumulates.

The architectural permanence of a converted building is itself a political argument — that these artists and their practices deserve the same stability that institutions extend to collections, not the contingency that most arts funding offers to living artists.

---

## Philosophical Approach

*"The art of staying."* Past remnants organically fusing with present visions. The space as a continually evolving living artwork — not a monument to what was, but infrastructure for what is being built.

---

## Connection to the Larger System

Every other project in this portfolio exists as software, performance, or text. MEKENA is the only one you can walk into.

This matters for institutional audiences — foundations, municipal arts bodies, real estate partners — who need to see Systems Choreography applied to the built environment, not just to code. MEKENA demonstrates that the methodology works at the scale of a building: that thinking about space as a system subject to movement and rehearsal produces different outcomes than treating it as a fixed container.

The same person who designed a generative memory engine also renovated a 1920s building in Queens to reduce its energy consumption by 30% while programming it as a refuge for displaced artists. The intellectual continuity between those two projects is the point.
