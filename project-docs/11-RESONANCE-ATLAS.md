**Tier:** Secondary Project

---

## What It Is

Every research program needs a name that tells you what it's an atlas of. The Resonance Atlas maps the territory between what sound can do and what current AI systems think sound is.

That gap — between the full range of human sonic experience and the narrower range that training data has taught machines to recognize — is the subject of this investigation.

The Atlas is not a single project. It is the conceptual umbrella under which a set of related research threads find their common framework. Individually, these threads are legible. Under the Atlas framing, they become a research program — something with a methodology, a set of questions, and a cumulative argument.

> *The question is not whether AI can make music. It already can. The question is whether the music it makes is for everyone — or for the narrow demographic whose music it has been trained to recognize as music at all.*

## Research Threads

| Thread | Core Question | Output |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| **The Localization Gap** | How do current generative audio models fail to represent non-Western musical traditions, and how can those failures be quantified? | 427-track adversarial audit; two failure taxonomies; white paper in preparation |
| **HAH-WAS Protocol** | What is the methodology for detecting, classifying, and reporting cultural hallucinations in generative AI systems? | Three-phase adversarial testing framework; reusable protocol across audio, text, and image modalities |
| **MaqamAI Research** | What ear training infrastructure would allow AI engineers and non-specialist reviewers to accurately evaluate generative Arabic music outputs? | Product concept for browser-based maqam ear training; integration design with HAH-WAS evaluation layer |
| **Computational Colonialism** | What is the theoretical framework for understanding AI training data bias as a continuation of historical power asymmetries in cultural representation? | Theoretical paper in development |
| **Algorithmic Reparations** | What would it mean to repair the cultural erasure embedded in AI training data — and what would the technical and institutional mechanisms look like? | Speculative framework; early-stage; long-term research agenda |

## The Unifying Argument

AI systems learn what they're taught. Their training data was assembled by humans with specific cultural backgrounds, institutional affiliations, and implicit definitions of quality — and the systems have inherited all of those assumptions as objective-seeming technical parameters.

The Western 12-TET tuning grid is not universal; it is hegemonic. Egyptian Arabic is not a proxy for Arabic; it is the most-represented Arabic in the training data. The difference between "it doesn't support this" and "it was never designed to support this" is the difference between a technical limitation and a cultural choice.

**The Resonance Atlas names this choice** and builds the tools to see it, measure it, and — eventually — correct it.

The work is not anti-AI. It is precisely *because* AI music tools are becoming the primary compositional infrastructure for large parts of the world's population that their cultural blind spots matter as much as they do. Indifference to this problem is not neutrality. It is a position.

## Who This Is For

1. **AI music companies** (Suno, Udio) — The localization gap represents a product quality problem with a direct market implication. MENA alone is 500M+ potential users. The Atlas research provides the diagnostic framework.

2. **AI researchers and ethicists** — The computational colonialism framework and algorithmic reparations agenda contribute to active academic discourse around bias, fairness, and cultural representation in machine learning.

3. **Cultural institutions and foundations** — Foundations investing in digital infrastructure for non-Western music traditions need to understand how current AI systems treat those traditions.

4. **Musicians and composers outside the Western tradition** — The most direct stakeholders. The Atlas research is a form of advocacy: making visible the degree to which "universal" tools are, in practice, not.
